Curatorial Strategy

Welcome to the Exhibition, "Exploring the Final Frontier: The Intersection of Culture and Science". To ensure that visitors leave with a memorable and immersive experience, I have incorporated several design choices. Following Dean’s advice, which advocates for a “logical, sequential organization” in exhibitions (1994, pg. 80), the exhibit has been laid out linearly, with displays sorted chronologically from the date each space craft project began. Following the advice outlined in Wallace (2014, pg. 5), it is essential that visitors are told a story through the chosen gallery artifacts. I aim to give visitors a narrative that first begins with wonder and curiosity, leads to collaboration and collective action, and finally leads to accomplishment and new discoveries.

The exhibit starts upon entrance, where they are immediately surrounded by audio that resembles the mechanical sounds from a distant spacecraft, contributing to the mood of fascination and awe, and the feeling of uneasiness of being in a vast space with no known bounds. To further immerse the visitor into this “outer space” setting, the lighting in the exhibit is nearly pitch black, with only the displays and explanatory frames illuminated. This lighting arrangement focuses visitors’ attention on the objects, enhances the futuristic and otherworldy ambiance, and recreates the feeling of being in space.

The objects on display are projected using a device propped up on a table, accompanied by VR eyewear that allows the visitors to interact with each object. It mimics 3D space where visitors can handle the object, while allowing the ability to manipulate the object’s size and viewing angles. Since visitors are masked with eyewear, there are no seating arrangements due to tripping hazards. Through VR eyewear, visitors gain the ability to zoom in, change the angle, and examine detailed features of each object, which encourages visitors’ engagement. A “real-life” experience is augmented through this website, which allows users to point, click, and drag their mouse within the display. I believe this keeps with the theme of how technology is embedded in our everyday lives, and sometimes even replaces physical realities.

Thematic to the “futuristic” design of the exhibition is the use of monospace fonts which resembles texts commonly seen in sci-fi movies. The display text beside each exhibit object is designed to look like a virtual interface. Each display contains an explanation of the objects from the perspective of material culture. The use of VR and interactive digital platforms, such as websites, is taken from Blais’s “Text in the Exhibition Medium” (1995, pg. 6) to offer new ways of interpreting and engaging with material culture from exhibitions.
